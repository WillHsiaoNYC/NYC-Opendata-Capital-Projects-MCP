Schedules × Budgets
the many-to-many anatomy of the capital portfolio

Every active project carries two identities. A PID is a construction schedule — what gets built, and when. An FMS ID is a budget line — where the money comes from. They are not the same thing, and they do not line up one-for-one. This is a map of how they connect, and of how $158 billion is distributed across them.

01

Mostly one-to-one — but the exceptions matter

Across the 3,001 PIDs and 2,993 budget lines active in period 202601, there are 3,125 schedule↔budget links. The vast majority are clean 1:1 pairs. The interesting ~3% fan out — one schedule drawing on several budgets, or one budget bankrolling many schedules. Whenever an ID resolves to multiple counterparts, all of them count; none is silently dropped.

03

The tangled few

The extremes are programmatic. On the budget side, a handful of NYPD city-wide lines (ADA compliance, general police facilities) each bankroll a dozen-plus separate site schedules. On the schedule side, a few complex projects are stitched together from up to five distinct funding sources. And these tangled PIDs are not small — they average $74M versus $40M for single-budget schedules.

04

Who tangles, and who stays clean

Thirteen managing agencies execute the 3,001 active schedules. Decomposing the fan-out by executor reveals two opposite behaviors. The construction-manager agencies — DDC and EDC — stitch single schedules together from many budgets (split funding). NYPD does the reverse: a few city-wide budget lines bankroll dozens of precinct sites (shared budgets). Almost everyone else runs an essentially perfect one-to-one shop.

Where the money lives

Budget lines sorted into size bands at the (managing agency × FMS ID) grain. Toggle the view and watch the distribution invert: by count the portfolio is a sea of small lines; by dollars it is a handful of giants. This is the single most important thing to understand about the capital budget.
